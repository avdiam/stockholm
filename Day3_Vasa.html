Museum Guide · Early Morning

Vasa Museum

The best single museum experience in Stockholm: a 69-metre, 98%-original 17th-century warship, covered stem to stern in sculpture, standing six storeys tall. It sank on its maiden voyage in 1628 and spent 333 years on the seabed. There is nothing else like it in the world.

The Short Answer

Is it worth it? Unreservedly yes.

Most history museums show you fragments and ask you to imagine the whole. The Vasa delivers the whole thing — the actual ship. Ordered by King Gustav II Adolf, it carried 64 bronze cannon on two gun decks and a crew of ~450. On 10 August 1628 it sailed barely 1,300 metres before a gust caught its sails and it capsized in the harbour, in full view of the shore. The cold, low-salinity Baltic preserved it for 333 years; it was salvaged in 1961. 98% of what you see is original. 460 sculptures and 300 ornaments cover it — once vivid red, blue, yellow and gold leaf.

Where to Focus

~1h 50m (08:40–10:30) — spend it like this

  1. The ship — go up to Levels 5–6 first ~40 min · top priority
    The stern sculptures up close (Level 5, best photos) and the full overview from the top, then descend slowly. Most people rush up and linger too little at the top.
  2. The ~20-min intro film ~20 min · watch early
    Construction, sinking and salvage — makes everything afterwards click. If the first showing is mid-run, take a first look at the ship and catch the next.
  3. The free 25-min English guided tour ~25 min · best value
    Guides surface the politics of the sculptures, the physics of the capsizing, and the skeleton archaeology. Check the board for times.
  4. "In Life and Death" + "Face to Face" ~10 min · don't skip
    The crew, reconstructed — quiet and genuinely moving (meet Filip).
  5. "Vasa Up Close" ~5 min if time
    The sculptures in their original blazing colours — changes how you read the ship.
If you're running short: drop "Vasa Up Close" — the ship itself and the skeleton rooms are the priority. The recommended sequence is Film → guided tour → roam the levels, so you get structure before you wander.
For the kid: the "Life on Board" objects (shoes, games, tools, an hourglass) and the "Ships in Battle" section (models, cannon-loading) are the most hands-on. The skeleton rooms suit most ages but the vivid faces may prompt questions.
On the Ship — and What to Look At

The details that stop you cold

⛵ The stern (19.3 m, all sculpture)

The first thing you see on entering, and the thing you photograph from Level 5. → Look for King David and Gideon (Old Testament) beside Hercules and the Nereids (Greek myth) — religion, mythology and royal propaganda mixed on purpose.

🦁 The lion figurehead (bow)

Over 3 metres long, gilded, snarling — the symbol of Sweden and of the king. → Get the low ground-level angle looking up the bow.

⚓ The gun ports & the waterline

Two full rows of cannon. → Note how close the lower gun deck sits to the waterline — top-heavy and fatally unstable; this is literally why she sank.

🏛️ The stern galleries & Roman emperors

The officers' ornate windows and balconies look like a Baroque palace afloat. Along the upper works, Roman emperors place Gustav II Adolf in the line of imperial Rome.

🎭 The shamed Polish nobleman

A carved figure on the bow designed to humiliate Sweden's enemies and stiffen the crew's courage — the politics of 1628 carved in wood.

The Six Viewing Levels

A circuit of the ship at six heights

LevelWhat you see
Ground (1)Full broadside — the scale of the hull hits you. Gun ports; the fatal waterline.
Level 2Close on the lower gun deck and cannon muzzles; original anchors and rigging.
Level 3Mid-ship detail — planking, joints, the texture of 400-year-old oak.
Level 4Eye level with the upper gun deck windows and mid-ship carvings.
Level 5The stern sculptures at close range — best photography level.
Level 6 (top)Down onto the open upper deck and across the whole ship — best sense of the form.
Tip: go up first, spend real time on Levels 5–6, then work back down slowly. Lifts reach every floor.
The Surrounding Exhibitions

Context wrapped around the ship

💀 "In Life and Death" + "Face to Face"

Two adjacent exhibitions on the ~25 people found aboard. In Life and Death reconstructs who they were (DNA, osteology, isotopes — age, diet, origin, even faces); Face to Face shows the skeletons with multimedia on their roles. → Find Filip (individual F) — a real sailor from 1628, not a symbol. Quiet and moving.

🎨 "Vasa Up Close"

Full-scale colour reconstructions of the sculptures as they originally looked — crimson, gold, vivid blue. The contrast with the dark ship is startling.

🥄 Life on Board

Wooden chests, pewter spoons, leather shoes, backgammon pieces, a comb, coins, an hourglass — the everyday texture of 17th-century seafaring, preserved on the seabed.

🌊 The Sinking & The Salvage

Why she went down (top-heavy design, miscalculation, and a king who demanded she sail before she was safe), and how a 1,200-tonne ship was raised intact from 32 m of water in 1961.

⚔️ Ships in Battle

Scale models, cannon-loading demos and Sweden's role as a Baltic sea power — the most interactive section, good for kids.
