What Kind of Place Is This?

Inspiring stories, told through objects

The Nobel Prize Museum tells the history of the Nobel Prizes through the personal objects, films, and voices of the laureates themselves. It is not an art gallery or a grand historic house — it is compact, thoughtfully curated, and rewards curiosity broadly. The Literature and Peace stories are as compelling as the science ones, and the objects make abstract achievements feel human.

It sits in the old Stock Exchange building (Börshuset), built 1773 — one of the finest Baroque facades in Stockholm, and free to admire from the square whether or not you go in.

⭐ Why Friday is the right window

Friday (Day 1) is the only day the museum stays open until 21:00. You're already at Stortorget at 17:35, and dinner isn't until 19:20 — a natural 30–60 minute detour with no backtracking. On Day 5 (Tuesday) the museum closes at 18:00 with no free or discounted entry, so this is the week's best slot. If the travel day has worn everyone out, skip the interior — the square and the evening walk are the priority.

What You'll See

Highlights

🪑 The Ceiling Conveyor

The museum's most distinctive sight: chairs from the Nobel banquet constantly cycle overhead on a dry-cleaning-style conveyor, each tagged with a laureate's name. Hundreds of chairs, slowly circling. It sounds gimmicky; in person it's unexpectedly moving — all those names passing above you.

🏅 "These Things Changed the World"

The permanent exhibition — over 200 artefacts from Nobel laureates: notebooks, instruments, manuscripts, letters, and tools, across all six categories (Physics, Chemistry, Medicine, Literature, Peace, Economics). Deliberately personal rather than technical: a novelist's draft page, a scientist's worn lab notebook, a peace activist's handmade badge.

🖥️ Interactive Screens

Browse every Nobel laureate since 1901 by year, country, category, gender, nationality, or age at the time of the prize. Good for a curious kid and for adults hunting a specific name.

🎬 The Film

A short introductory film on Alfred Nobel and the prizes, looping in the cinema — worth 15 minutes if you arrive as one is starting.

🍦 Bistro Nobel

The café serves the Nobel ice cream — a flavour created for the Nobel banquet, slightly different each year. A small thing, but a genuine treat if your son spots it.

🧒 Children's Room / Workshop

Hands-on experiments, a quiz, and activities for younger visitors — handy if anyone needs a 10-minute break from the exhibitions.

Is It Worth It?

Yes — but calibrate to energy levels

  • Two adults = 320 SEK (~€29) total (your son is free under 18).
  • 45–75 minutes sees everything without rushing.
  • The ceiling conveyor, personal objects, and interactive screens all land well with mixed-age groups.
  • The Börshuset exterior on Stortorget is free and worth a moment regardless.

The one honest caveat: some visitors feel the content is thin relative to the price. Go in expecting inspiring stories told through objects, not a sprawling gallery, and it delivers.

Suggested Route (If You Go)

A 30–60 min flow into the evening walk

17:35 — Arrive at Stortorget
Photograph the square first (~5 min) — one of Stockholm's great spaces — and the Börshuset facade.
17:40 — Enter the museum
Buy at the desk (card only). Check the film times posted at the door.
17:45 — The film
~15 min if one is starting — or skip straight to the permanent exhibition.
18:00 — Permanent exhibition & ceiling conveyor
~25 min. Let the phone audio guide — or just the objects — lead the way.
~18:25 — Exit to fika
Two-minute walk to Café Schweizer, Västerlånggatan 9.

If you skip the museum entirely: photograph the Börshuset facade from the square — one of Stockholm's finest 18th-century buildings — and head to Café Schweizer at 18:00.
