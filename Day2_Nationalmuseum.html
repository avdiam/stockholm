What Kind of Place Is This?

A world-class collection, read like a timeline

About 5,000 works hang chronologically along a timeline that climbs through the building: European Old Masters on the top floor, 19th-century & Impressionism on the floor below, and Design on the ground floor. For your taste the two strongest zones are the top floor (Rembrandt, the great French 18th-century rooms) and the level below (Renoir and the Swedish "Opponents"). It's big — plan to beeline the headliners rather than walk every gallery.

Where to Focus

~1h45m (entry ~14:30) — spend it like this

  1. Top floor — Old Masters first ~50 min · top priority
    Rembrandt's Claudius Civilis & Kitchen Maid → Rubens / El Greco → Cranach / Arcimboldo → the French 18th-c. rooms (Watteau, Chardin, Boucher) → Roslin's Lady with the Veil.
  2. Down one level — Impressionism & 19th-c. ~60 min
    Beeline to Renoir's La Grenouillère, then Degas / Manet / Gauguin → the Swedish Opponents (Zorn, Larsson, Liljefors).
  3. Optional wind-down (~16:30) ~15 min if time
    The Sergel exhibition (floor 4) or the free ground-floor design galleries. Be out of the upper galleries by ~16:50.
If you're running short: drop the wind-down buffer — the Rembrandt / El Greco / Renoir / Zorn headliners still fit in ~95 minutes. Skip the Design ground floor (it's free, so you can dip in any time before close).
With a kid: Arcimboldo's fruit-and-vegetable portraits up top are an easy crowd-pleaser, and the free "Colour!" children's exhibition is a good short bonus if energy allows.
Highlights — and What to Look At

The headliners, by floor

Top floor · European Old Masters (~1500–1800)

🖼️ Rembrandt — The Conspiracy of the Batavians under Claudius Civilis (1661–62)

The museum's single most important painting — a monumental surviving fragment of the huge canvas Rembrandt painted for Amsterdam's Town Hall and that was rejected and cut down. → Look at how the light pools on the conspirators' swords meeting at the centre; this is late-Rembrandt at his most radical and rough-brushed.

🕯️ Rembrandt — The Kitchen Maid (1651)

An intimate chiaroscuro study and a family favourite. → Note how she leans on the window-sill straight out toward you — warmth and immediacy from almost nothing.

🥦 Arcimboldo — Vertumnus (1591) & The Jurist (1566)

The composite "fruit-and-vegetable" portraits — instant kid crowd-pleasers. → Step close, then back: the faces dissolve into produce and reassemble.

🎀 French 18th-century rooms — Watteau, Chardin, Boucher

A world-class holding: Watteau's The Love Lesson, Chardin's quiet still lifes, Boucher's The Triumph of Venus (1740). → Slow down here — this suite is one of the museum's real strengths.

👰 Roslin — The Lady with the Veil (1768)

Often called the museum's most beloved painting (the artist's wife). → Look at the black lace veil over her face — the virtuoso passage everyone remembers. El Greco's Apostles Peter and Paul and Rubens' Bacchus and Ariadne are nearby.

Floor below · 19th-century & Impressionism

🌊 Renoir — La Grenouillère (1869)

The Impressionist star of the museum — Renoir's shimmering riverside bathing scene, painted side-by-side with Monet at the spot that effectively launched Impressionism. → Get close to see the broken, dabbed brushwork on the water, then step back to watch it cohere.

🎨 Degas, Manet, Gauguin & Delacroix

All represented (specific canvases rotate — confirm on the day's floor map). Delacroix's The Lion Hunt (1855) is on the Romantic stretch en route.

🇸🇪 The Swedish "Opponents" — don't skip

A genuine strength: Zorn — Midsummer Dance (1897); Carl Larsson — the mural-scale Midwinter Sacrifice (1915); plus Liljefors (wildlife), Prince Eugen, Josephson's The Water Sprite and Hanna Pauli's Breakfast Time. → Larsson's huge canvas is unmissable; Zorn's light is the thing to look for.

Bonus · Temporary exhibitions (in the ticket)

🗿 "Fantasy and Reality — The Art of Johan Tobias Sergel"

19 Feb – 10 Aug 2026, floor 4. Sweden's master Neoclassical sculptor — ~400 works incl. terracotta sketches and rarely-shown drawings. Very on-theme if you have spare minutes.

🧒 "Colour! — for children and their curious grown-ups"

A hands-on children's exhibition about colour — a good short family bonus.

🏺 "Wilhelm Kåge & Shōji Hamada"

Swedish/Japanese studio ceramics — a small design-floor detour for the design-curious.
