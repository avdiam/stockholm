What Kind of Place Is This?

A dense little royal treasury

A royal store of ceremonial armour, court costume, weapons, and carriages spanning 500 years of Swedish monarchy. For your taste, the draw is the Renaissance parade armour — among the finest preserved anywhere — and a handful of objects so charged with history they're unforgettable (a king's battle horse; the clothes he died in). It's small and dense: you're here for a dozen star objects, not endless galleries.

Highlights — and What to Look At

The dozen things to see

🛡️ Erik XIV's parade armour & shield (1562)

The Renaissance treasure. Iron forged in Arboga, Sweden; sent to Antwerp to be embossed, chased, etched and gilt by goldsmith Eliseus Libaerts after designs by the French Mannerist engraver Étienne Delaune. Purely ceremonial — designed to dazzle. → Look closely at the shield's embossed mythological scenes and the etched-and-gilt detailing — this is goldsmith's work, not a soldier's kit.

⚔️ Karl IX's armour

Etched-and-embossed steel with applied gold; same Arboga-forged / Antwerp-decorated pedigree. → Compare its decoration with Erik XIV's to see the house style a generation on.

🐎 The horse Streiff

Gustav II Adolf's actual battle steed from Lützen (1632), where the king was killed. ~400 years old; one of very few preserved royal warhorses anywhere — the museum's iconic object.

🩸 Gustav II Adolf's Lützen clothing

The bloodstained garments he wore when he fell; preserving them is literally why the museum exists. → See this and Streiff as a pair — together they tell the Lützen story.

🎭 Gustav III's masquerade costume

Worn the night he was shot at a masked ball (1792). → The assassin Anckarström's mask and pistols are displayed here too.

👑 Royal robes & Karl XII's coat

Queen Christina's purple-velvet coronation mantle (Paris-made), Sofia Magdalena's silver-brocade wedding dress (1766, 3-metre train), and Karl XII's coat and hat — → Look for the visible bullet hole from his death (1718).

🛻 Five gilded royal carriages

One staircase down in the Carriage Hall (incl. the Coronation Carriage). Lowest priority for your taste — see them only if time allows.
