What Kind of Place Is This?

A modernist collection that rotates hard

Moderna holds a world-class modern collection — anchored by a superb Surrealism and Duchamp holding — much of it gathered in the 1963 "Museum of Our Wishes" campaign. The catch: the collection rotates heavily, so any given painting may be in storage. The way to make a short late-afternoon visit reliably satisfying is to anchor it on the always-accessible outdoor works plus the confirmed-indoor Dalí and Monogram, and treat the rest as a bonus you confirm at the desk.

Where to Focus

~45–55 min (17:05–18:00) — spend it like this

  1. Dalí — The Enigma of William Tell (1933) ~10 min · top priority
    Confirmed on view in the Surrealism room ("A Meeting of Opposites") — the single most important painting in the building.
  2. Rauschenberg — Monogram (1955–59) ~8 min
    The goat with a tyre; confirmed in Study Gallery 08. The museum's emblem.
  3. The outdoor anchors ~10 min · free, always there
    Picasso's concrete Déjeuner sur l'herbe, Niki de Saint Phalle & Tinguely's Le Paradis fantastique, and Calder's The Four Elements — catch these on the way out.
  4. Bonus indoor (if on view) ~10 min if time
    Duchamp's Bicycle Wheel, Miró, Léger, Brancusi, Oppenheim's fur cup — ask the desk which are up.
Verify first: Picasso's Joueur de guitare and Matisse's Apollon are both flagged not on display as of June 2026 — don't promise a kid the cut-out collage. Ask at the desk: "Is Study Gallery 08 open, and is anything from Picasso/Matisse on view today?"
The reassuring part: even if every rotating painting is out, the outdoor Picasso, Niki de Saint Phalle and Calder are free and always there — so the visit can't be a wash. Great with a kid.
Highlights — and What to Look At

The headliners

Guaranteed · confirmed on view June 2026

🎯 Dalí — The Enigma of William Tell (1933) On view

The crown-jewel Surrealist painting — huge (201.5 × 346 cm); Dalí cast his own father and Lenin as Tell. Nearly never leaves the museum; likely the most important Dalí outside Spain/the US. → Look at the elongated buttock propped on a crutch — Dalí's deliberate provocation aimed at his father. In the "A Meeting of Opposites" Surrealism room.

🐐 Rauschenberg — Monogram (1955–59) Study Gallery 08

A stuffed Angora goat girdled by a car tyre, standing on a painted board — the "combine" that fuses painting and sculpture, and the emblem of the whole museum. → Walk around it; it's meant to be seen in the round.

🌿 Picasso — Déjeuner sur l'herbe (1962, concrete) Outdoors

A monumental sandblasted concrete sculpture group on the lawn (executed by Carl Nesjar). Outdoors, always free, always on view — the guaranteed must-see Picasso.

🎠 Niki de Saint Phalle & Tinguely — Le Paradis fantastique (1966) Outdoors

Colourful bulbous figures playing against Tinguely's black machines — outdoors, free, always there. A big family favourite. Calder's The Four Elements (1961) standing mobile is outdoors too.

Bonus indoor · confirm at the desk

🚲 Duchamp — Bicycle Wheel (1913/1960) + Rotoreliefs

Part of the best Duchamp holding outside Philadelphia. → The original readymade idea, paired with his spinning optical discs.

☕ Meret Oppenheim — Object / fur cup & saucer (1936)

The fur-covered teacup — Surrealism's most famous object. Verify which version is up.

🖼️ Brancusi, Miró, Léger, Mondrian, de Chirico

Brancusi's Newborn II, Miró's Red Figure, Léger's The Staircase, a Mondrian Composition, a de Chirico metaphysical cityscape — a strong early-modernist roll-call, rotation permitting.

🚫 Off-view as of June 2026 Check

Picasso's Joueur de guitare (1916, the flagship Cubist canvas) and Matisse's Apollon (1953, the big cut-out collage) are both flagged not on display. Don't count on them. Note: there's no Picasso "Spring" here, and the famous Munch oils / The Scream are in Oslo — Moderna's Munch is mainly prints.
