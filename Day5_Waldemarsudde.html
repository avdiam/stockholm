Prince Eugen's Waldemarsudde

A royal painter's home, a world-class collection, and a sculpture garden above the water. Prince Eugen (1865–1947) was a prince who painted seriously — and collected obsessively. He left his house, studio, garden and ~7,000 works to Sweden. The same hand that painted the City Hall mural you saw yesterday is everywhere here.

The Short Answer

One of Stockholm's most rewarding art experiences.

Prince Eugen, fourth son of King Oscar II, studied painting in Paris in the 1880s and became a respected landscape painter of the specifically Nordic light — grey-blue water, long summer evenings, melancholic forest. He used his royal wealth to back artists before they were famous, assembling one of the finest private collections of late-19th/early-20th-century Nordic and French art anywhere. Part gallery, part lived-in house, part sculpture park on the water — and a direct sequel to yesterday's City Hall, where his mural Stockholm's Shores shows the same view he painted from this terrace dozens of times.

Where to Focus

~1h 55m (12:20–14:15) — spend it like this

  1. The French & Swedish "Opponents" in the gallery ~30 min · the heart
    Cézanne, Pissarro, Degas, then Bergh, Nordström, Zorn, Josephson — the core of what makes this collection world-class.
  2. Prince Eugen's own landscapes ~20 min · the link to Day 4
    His Stockholm-across-the-water paintings and Nordic forests. Hold the City Hall mural in mind — same hand, same view.
  3. The garden & terrace ~30 min · don't skip in good weather
    Rodin's The Thinker above the straits, Bourdelle's Heracles, the Nike copy, the ancient Prince's Oak. As rewarding as the interior.
  4. The mansion / private apartments ~20 min · the contrast
    His study, books and effects — domestic scale after the gallery, and the rooms feel lived-in, not reconstructed.
  5. The current shows if time
    In Bloom – Art & Botany and Prince Plants pair nicely with the garden right outside.
If you're short on time: the garden and Prince Eugen's own paintings are the irreplaceable parts — they exist only here. Trim the temporary shows before the permanent collection. In poor weather, flip it: more gallery, less garden.
For the kid: the garden wins — Rodin's Thinker and Bourdelle's giant archer above the water, plus the open lawns and the path to the water's edge after a morning indoors at Skansen.
The Collection — and What to Look For

What hangs on these walls

🎨 Prince Eugen's own paintings

The museum's heart. → Look for the recurring Stockholm skyline seen across the water from Djurgården, in his grey-blue-green palette — the private twin of the public City Hall mural. His spruce-and-birch forest paintings are equally strong.

🇸🇪 Swedish "Opponents" & National Romantics

The same generation you met at Nationalmuseum, collected by a peer. → Seek out Richard Bergh (his closest friend, most deeply represented), Karl Nordström (the most-represented artist, ~50 works), Anders Zorn, and the turbulent Ernst Josephson.

🇫🇷 French art

From his Paris years and lifelong ties. → Cézanne (among the most valuable works here), Pissarro, Corot, Degas, plus smaller Matisse and Gauguin pieces and Bastien-Lepage, who shaped the whole Swedish generation.

🖌️ Munch & the avant-garde

Edvard Munch as a fellow Nordic symbolist — strong works on paper rather than the famous Oslo oils — alongside Picasso and Derain representing the early-20th-century Paris avant-garde.

The Garden — Sculpture Above the Water

70,000 m² of grounds

🗿 Rodin — The Thinker

One of Prince Eugen's early purchases, set on the main terrace flanking the house. → Stand so the figure reads against the Stockholm straits behind — the single best image of the visit.

🏹 Bourdelle — Heracles the Archer

North of the house, drawing the bow against the Stymphalian birds — by Rodin's most important student. Powerful and dynamic up close.

🪽 Nike of Samothrace (copy)

Built into the stair design when the house went up in 1905 — a bronze echo of the Louvre original. Plus Swedish sculptors Carl Milles and Carl Eldh throughout the grounds.

🌳 The Prince's Oak & Ekotemplet

An ancient oak among Djurgården's oldest trees, and a small garden pavilion (Ekotemplet) — a quiet spot with views. Walk to the water's edge if time allows.

Practical Notes

🔗 The Day 4 connection

After the Prince's Gallery mural at City Hall, recognising the same hand and the same subject — Stockholm's waterfront — here is one of the trip's most satisfying moments. Same painter, public vs private.

📸 Photography

Permitted in the gallery and garden. The terrace, with Rodin and the water behind, is the best single shot.

☕ After

5-min walk to Café Ektorpet (14:15–14:55 fika), then ~20-min walk north along Djurgårdsstrand to Restaurant Eld for the 15:15 booking.
